Imperfect Subjunctive

Last chapter we learned the first of four subjunctive mood tenses.  The imperfect tense is going to be used in subordinate clauses to express things that are happening in the past.  This will not be the ONLY subjunctive verb tense that does this.  In a few chapters we’ll learn the other one.  There is actually a set of rules for when it is appropriate to use this tense versus others, but this topic will be tackled in chapter 18.      

Let’s start as we usually do and learn the forms of this verb tense.  Remember, the imperfect tense has already been introduced many chapters ago, but that was for the indicative mood.  The rules for the imperfect subjunctive are different.  You’ll be happy to learn that the rules for this tense are the SAME across all conjugations, unlike the present subjunctive.  This includes the rules for “sum, esse”.

IMPERFECT SUBJUNCTIVE

Infinitive + endings

Ex: amare + t

amaret

It is so simple and easy.

Adverbial Result

What are adverbial result clauses? These clauses, often called CONSECUTIVE clauses, as the name suggests, set out what happened because of some previous action or event - in other words, their RESULT!

It will be much easier to understand if we look at some examples, first in English:

    e.g.  The soldier ran so quickly that no-one could catch him

            There were so many arrows that our army was overwhelmed

                The nymph is so beautiful that Apollo is in love with her

You will quickly notice a certain "formula" of expression common to each of the examples above:

           

"SO (+ an adjective or adverb) ……THAT……."

Many of the adjectives and adverbs that are used in these sentences are vocabulary words in this chapter.  These words can be used to INDICATE that an adverbial result clause is present as opposed to a purpose clause.  These words would be wise to memorize:

Tam     Tot     Totiens  Ita        Sic     Tantus

Adeo   Talis

This list is not exhaustive, and in fact the example in this chapter DOES NOT use one of these words.  In addition to this indicator word, the rest of the requirements to have this type of clause are “ut” followed by a subjunctive verb.  The adverbial result can look extremely similar to purpose clauses.  There are two big differences to look for.  The first is the indicator word discussed above.  The second thing to look out for is that a NEGATIVE adverbial result does NOT use the word “ne”.  It uses “ut...non” instead.  

Adverbial Result

Indicator word + ut/ut...non + subjunctive verb

Let’s take a look at Basic Sentence 16.4 to see an example in action.

mons autem altissimus impendebat, [ut facile perpauci prohibere possent.]  

The mountain, however, loomed so tall [that a few people are able to block (us).]

The translator has to take a few liberties to read this sentence and have it make sense in clear English.  The major liberty that needs to be taken is with the word “altissimus”.  Normally this word would be translated as “highest”, but as you might see, there is a clause that is about to begin with the word “ut”.  This clause would not make sense as a purpose clause; a mountain doesn’t loom with any purpose in mind.  What would make sense is that a mountain might loom/hang over a pass in such a way as to prohibit troop movement.  This causes the reader to reassess how to read the word “altissimus”.  You might see that the translation that I went with, “so tall”, has a relation with some of the indicator words required for an adverbial result clause.

Fearing Clause

In English, as in Latin, an infinitive can be used where one is stating a fact in relation to fear:

Timebant silvam intrare.

They were afraid to enter the forest.

There is nothing new to learn there.  The infinitive use is very natural in our own language.  Fear can also be expressed in a simple sentence with no subordinate verb clause:

Caesar consensum Gallorum timuit.

Caesar feared the union of the Gauls.

However, verbs and phrases that express fear of an anticipated situation are followed by a subjunctive clause. This is called a clause of fearing. The subjunctive clause is introduced by ut or ne. This type of construction gives insight into the speaker's attitude towards the anticipated condition:

Timeo ne cadat

I fear that he may/will fall (i.e., I hope he does NOT fall)

Timeo ut cadat = I fear that he may/will not fall (i.e., I hope that he DOES fall).

Notice how the last two sentences are translated. What you would expect after ut and ne is REVERSED. That's the trickiest part of such clauses. Thinking of "fear" as a form of indirect wish may help you to remember this reversal of these two introductory words.

ut = HOPE it will happen = FEAR it may NOT happen

ne = HOPE it will NOT happen = FEAR it may happen

In English attention is concentrated on the positive or negative of what might happen, whereas in Latin one focuses on the positive or negative concept of what one wishes and anticipates. Thus, what the speaker fears may happen is introduced by ne. What he fears may not happen is introduced by ut.  Sometimes a subjunctive clause of fearing uses ne... non... in place of ut. This results in a double negative:

Metuo ne hodie grammaticus non me interroget.

I fear that the teacher may fail to call on me today.

So, to put all of this in a nutshell, a fearing clause consists of the following:

Clause of Fearing

Verb of fearing + ut/ne (reversed) + subjunctive verb

The Basic Sentence dealing with this clause is long and convoluted, but it contains all of the necessary elements.

Defertur ea res ad Caesarem. Ille veritus, [quod ad plures pertinebat], [ne civitas eorum impulsu deficeret], Lucium Plancum cum legione ex Belgio celeriter in Carnutes proficisci iubet.

This news was delivered to Caesar.  He, afraid, [because it pertained to many (people)], [that their state would rebel with an attack], ordered Lucius Plancus to set out quickly to the Carnutes (people) with a legion from Belgium.

Our fearing clause here is “that their state would rebel with an attack.”  It was set up by the verb (here a participle of the verb “vereor”) “veritus” and then the subordinating conjunction “ne”.

On the list of clauses you’ll need to remember, the clause of fearing is on the rare side.  It also will usually draw attention to itself by the verb of fearing, so it would be important to remember the handful of verb that fit into this semantic category.

Noun Result

Noun result clauses, also called substantive result clauses, is a clause which is either the subject or object of a verb of causing or happening.  In English this can be done too: With my homework completed it came about that I aced the test.  Notice that that subject of the verb “come about” seems to be the word “it”, however, it is the entire clause “that I aced the test”.

Verbs that start noun result clauses:

Efficio

Facio

Fio

Accido

If you see any of these verbs followed by an ut/ne, then you have a noun result clause.

Noun Result

Special verb(above) + ut/ne + subjunctive verb

A close cousin to this clause is another clause which some call a namely that cause (adjectival “ut” clause).  Instead of standing in for the subject or object of the sentence, the “ut” clause can be adjectival describing a noun.  In these instances you would NOT see a special verb and it would be indistinguishable from a purpose clause.  The context of the sentence would help you figure it out.  Here is an example from Caesar:

damnatum poenam sequi oportebat, [ut igni cremaretur].

It is fitting that punishment follow (him) having been condemned, [namely that he be burned with fire].

You’ll notice that the “ut” here is translated as “namely that” and is acting adjectivally describing the “poenam” or punishment.  

The reason that I have lumped these two clause types together is that some grammarians categorize noun result clauses as a type of namely that clause. For instance if you look at Basic Sentence 16.2, you can see that it fits the bill as having a noun result clause.  It has the word “accidit” followed by “ut” and finally containing a subjunctive verb.  It could however be read as a namely that clause and sound fine too.

Eadem nocte accidit [ut esset luna plena]….

On that same night it happened [that the moon was full]…  

(read as noun result.  Preferred in this instance)

On that same night it happened, [namely that the moon was full]...

(read a namely that clause)
