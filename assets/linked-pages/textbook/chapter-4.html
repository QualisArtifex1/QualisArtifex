The Genitive Case

The second to last main case is one of the most important partly because it is used frequently, but also because it is part of the dictionary entry of a noun that you have seen for 4 chapters now. In this chapter and in the future I will be denoting the genitive case in green.

The genitive case is BEST handled when you see it by translating the noun with the word "of" before it. Just as the ablative case, there are many different versions of these genitives that some grammarians give special names to. Keep in mind that as you read these terms below, they are ALL still using the word "of" to express the genitive.

Genitive types

1. Of Possession (the most common) In English this is sometimes expressed by adding an "'s" to a noun.

A good example of this is Basic Sentence 4.1.

Mercurius est nuntius deorum.
Mercury is the messenger of the gods.
Mercury is the gods' messenger.

The meaning is the same between the two interpretations. There is a video that might help elaborate a bit for you here.

2. Objective

Causa belli est ignota.
The cause of the war is unknown.

As you can see. This use still uses the word "of" but doesn't show possession.

3. Partitive

pars viae est recta.
part of the road is straight.

This example shows that if a genitive is showing that it is part of a larger whole then it is called a partitive genitive.

To sum up all this information it is best to go back to the basics. If you see a noun in the genitive, it is probably best to translate it using the word "of" before the noun and not worry so much at this point what kind of genitive it is. In fact, there are many more types that one day you may learn, but for now...KEEP IT SIMPLE.

Verb Conjugations 3 and 4

In chapter 3, you learned that a verb can be categorized into one of 4 conjugations. You learned that 1st conjugation verbs have an "a" in the stem and 2nd conjugation verbs have an "e" in the stem. The 3rd conjugation does not have a vowel in the stem.

At first glance, the 2nd and 3rd conjugations can look quite similar because both have an "e" in some of their forms. The way to tell them apart is by looking at the first two principal parts.

Video, videre, vidi, visum = 2nd conjugation because it has an "eo" in the first principal part.
Dico, dicere, dixi, dictum = 3rd conjugation because it has an "ere" infinitive and just an "o" in the first principal part.

Dico, dicere, dixi, dictumsingularplural
1st persondicodicimus
2nd persondicisdicitis
3rd persondicitdicunt

There is no rhyme or reason to it, but for 3rd conjugation verbs, think: O, 4 i's, U.

4th conjugation verbs are more straightforward. They have an "i" in the stem and an infinitive ending in "ire".

Venio, venire, veni, ventumsingularplural
1st personveniovenimus
2nd personvenisvenitis
3rd personvenitveniunt

The tricky form in 4th conjugation is the 3rd person plural, because it has a "u" after the "i": veniunt.

Causal Clause

A causal clause is a subordinate clause that gives the reason why something happens. In English, we often introduce this kind of clause with "because" or "since." Latin frequently uses quod for this same job.

Helvetii reliquos Gallos audaciā praecedunt, [quod cotidianis proeliis cum Germanis contendunt.]
The Helvetians precede the rest of the Gauls in boldness, [because they contend with the Germans in daily battles.]

The square brackets show the subordinate clause. It gives the cause or reason for the main statement.

The Latin Dictionary Entry

The way that a Latin word appears in the dictionary tells us a lot of information. We can use that information to determine what kind of word it is and how that word is going to behave in a sentence.

Nouns

When a noun is listed in the the dictionary you are given the following:

NOMINATIVE, GENITIVE   GENDER
Puella, puellae f

The gender abbreviation tells you whether the noun is masculine, feminine, or neuter. The genitive ending tells you which declension the noun belongs to and helps you find the stem of the noun.

Verbs

When a verb is listed in the dictionary you are usually given 4 principal parts. There is a video that explains these principal parts here.

1st PERSON SINGULAR, INFINITIVE, 3rd PART, 4th PART
Amo, amare, amavi, amatum

The first principal part is the 1st person singular form. The second principal part is the infinitive. There is also a video on infinitives here.

Amare = to love
Dicere = to speak
Habere = to have

The infinitive is especially useful because it helps identify the conjugation of the verb. The last two principal parts will become more important later, but they are included now so that you can begin recognizing the full dictionary entry.

All Other Words

All other words, such as adverbs, conjunctions, and prepositions, are usually indeclinable. That means they do not change form the way nouns, adjectives, and verbs do.
