Imperfect Tense

The imperfect tense describes an action that is going on in the past.  This action is usually continuous, repeated, or customary.  This tense in English is usually described using the past progressive tense.  Here are some examples of how this would look in English:

The neighbor was mowing the lawn.

When I was a kid, I used to play video games.

The reason why it is called imperfect is because the action is continuous and the end of that action either hasn’t happened, didn’t happen or is unknown.  If you look at the first English sentence above, we can assume that the neighbor was mowing the lawn over a period of time, but we are not sure if he ever finished.  Maybe he was interrupted or maybe not.  Having said all of this, Latin also does not have a simple past tense like English, and so from time to time Latin will use the imperfect like we use the simple past tense in English as well.

In Latin when you see an imperfect tense verb, you should translate that verb as was/were verbing or the simple past tense of verbed.

So, how do you form the imperfect tense in Latin?  This is where the image of the sheep from the beginning of the chapter comes in.  A sheep says “ba”.  If you take those letters and place them between the stem and the ending of a verb, then that is the imperfect form.  Let’s take a look at an example.

[Although the leaders and fortune were deserting them,] nevertheless they placed all hope of safety in (their) virtue.

Pronoun Morphology

A pronoun is a word that stands in for a noun.  Examples include: I, you, he, she, it, we, they, this, that, them, those etc. etc.  There are many.  The point of having a whole section in this book on pronouns is that they dance to their own tune. They want to act like nouns and have the same case endings, but sometimes they differ.  I would focus more on the differences between the ways nouns and pronouns are formed.  What you are about to look at is a bunch of charts, one for each pronoun, and after each chart is a short explanation of how they differ from the noun chart that you should already know.   Don’t be overwhelmed by the following charts!  Focus on the differences to make it easier.

This word is a REFLEXIVE PRONOUN.  What this means is that it “reflects” back onto the subject, whatever that might be.

Example:  

Puella se videt.  

The girl sees herself.  

Because the subject of this sentence is a girl, the word “se” is read as “herself”.  Notice there is no nominative because naturally it can’t reflect onto the subject if it IS the subject.

Locative Case

 Locative: The locative case is a case that only applies to the following types of words: Cities, towns, small islands, domus, rus, and humus.  That’s it. The locative is translated as “at” or “in” and is relatively rare.  It does NOT appear on a normal declension chart.

Let’s say I wanted to write “I am walking in the street” in Latin:

Ambulo in via

Easy enough.  Notice to express where I am walking, I use the ablative case after the word “in”.  However, if I wanted to say, “I am walking in Rome”, I have to use the locative case for the word “Rome” because it is a city, town, or small island:

Ambulo Romae.  +
