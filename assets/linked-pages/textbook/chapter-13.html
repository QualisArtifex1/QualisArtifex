Future Perfect Tense

The Future Perfect is formed in English with the auxiliary words WILL and HAVE, followed by the past participle of the main verb.  It is the rarest of verb tenses and the last one you need to learn!!:

By six pm tonight:

  • I will have finished this book
  • you will have studied the English tenses
  • he will have cooked dinner
  • he will have arrived
  • we will have met Julie
  • it will have stopped raining
  • they will have left Japan

The Future Perfect tense shows an action in the future that will be completed.  You may notice that in English it is just a combination of the Future (will) and the Perfect (have).

Future Active Participle (FAP)

In chapter 6 you learned the present active participle (PAP) and in chapter 11 you learned the perfect passive participle (PPP).  In this chapter you will be introduced to the last participle, the FAP.

The FAP stands for Future Active Participle and is relatively straightforward if you remember the rules for the PPP.  The FAP indicates an action that takes place in the future and is a verbal-adjective.  The noun that it is describing will need to match in case, number, and gender and its antecedent will be doing the action.

How to form a FAP:  You will take the PPP and add a “ur” right before adding 1st and 2nd declension adjective endings. This should be easy to remember because “ur” appears in the English word “futURe”.  So there are three pieces:  

amat + ur + (endings)

Adjective and Adverbs (Degree and Comparison)

Up to this point you have only seen adjectives as being differentiated as 1st/2nd declension adjectives or 3rd declension adjectives.  This will continue to be the case, however, you will soon learn that each adjective and their adverb counterparts have three degrees.  

In English, adjectives come in three forms, also called degrees. This will be the same in Latin.  An adjective in its normal or usual form is called a positive degree adjective. There are also the comparative and superlative degrees, which are used for comparison, as in the following examples:

Positive

Comparative

Superlative

sweet

sweeter

sweetest

bad

worse

worst

efficient

more efficient

most efficient

A common error in using adjectives and adverbs arises from using the wrong form of comparison. To compare two things, always use a comparative adjective:

Example: She is the cleverer of the two women (never cleverest)

The word cleverest is what is called the superlative form of clever. Use it only when comparing three or more things:

Example: She is the cleverest of them all.

There are also three degrees of adverbs as well. In formal usage, do not drop the -ly from an adverb when using the comparative form.

Incorrect: She spoke quicker than he did.

Correct: She spoke more quickly than he did.

Incorrect: Talk quieter.

Correct: Talk more quietly.

Superlative

Most quietly

Of course there are adjectives and adverbs that are irregular in English (good, better, best) and many of these irregular words will also be irregular in Latin.  These irregular words will have to be memorized separately from the rules that you are about to learn.

COMPARATIVE (MORE or -er)

Comparative adjectives are compared by adding -ior (M.&F.) or -ius (N.) to the base. The base is taken from the genitive singular of the adjective. The comparative adjective is then declined as a regular third declension.  These endings will take all the forms that 3rd declension adjectives can take. There are the normal 20 forms.

Comparative

Longior, longius

fortior, fortius

Superlative (MOST or -est)

Superlative adjectives are formed by adding normal 1st and 2nd declension endings (-us, -a, -um) to the base. The stem is taken from the genitive singular:   Stem + “issim” + 1st and 2nd declension endings.

Superlative

Longissimus, a, um

Fortissimus, a, um

Ablative of Comparison

To compare two things in Latin you have two options.  You can either use the word “quam” to mean “than” or you can leave “quam” out and use a N.I.A.W.O.P. of the word being compared.  If you use a N.I.A.W.O.P. (like the second example below) then this is called AN ABLATIVE OF COMPARISON.  Here are two sentences to illustrate this:

Puella est altior quam puer.

Puella est altior puero.

Both of these sentences mean the same thing: The girl is taller than the boy.

Adverbs

Adverbs in Latin are have all three degrees (positive, comparative, and superlative) just like their adjective counterparts.  Remember though that adverbs are indeclinable so once you learn the rules for adverbs then there will be only one form for each degree!

POSITIVE DEGREE

1st/2nd declension nouns

3rd declension nouns

add “e” to the stem

Add “iter” or “er” to the stem

Latus, a, um------> late

wide-------> widely

Fortis, forte------> fortiter

brave-----> bravely

COMPARATIVE DEGREE

Take the neuter nominative form (same as neuter adjective)

Latior, latius ------> latius

wider-----> more widely

SUPERLATIVE DEGREE

Add “e” to the superlative stem

Latissimus, a, um -----> latissime

widest-----> most widely

Let’s take a look at at least one Basic Sentence to see all of these forms in action

13.4 'Rex erat Aeneas nobis, quo iustior alter,

nec pietate fuit, nec bello maior et armis.    

Aeneas was king to us, than whom no other [was] more just 

Neither was [anyone] better in piety nor in war and arms.  

You might notice that there are a number of words that are left out in the Latin and understood to be there.  This is common in real Latin text.  Also notice the use of the new comparative adjectives “iustior” and “maior”.  These adjectives are nominative and are describing Aeneas in a linking sentence as predicate adjectives.  These comparative adjectives also set up the new use for N.I.A.W.O.P.s, the ablative of comparison(quo, pietate, bello, and armis).
