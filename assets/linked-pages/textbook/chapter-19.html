Gerunds

Gerunds are verbals nouns.  Their roots come from the stems of verbs but they will take case endings and function as a noun.  Gerunds are always ACTIVE in meaning and they will never take the nominative case in Latin.  Because of their active/verbal nature, they can take direct objects like verbs too!  To understand a gerund more let’s look first as to how to look in English.  Gerunds end in “-ing” but this might be confusing because present active participles also ending in “-ing”, so care must be taken.  The way to tell the difference is whether or not the word is being used as a noun.  

I have been told that skydiving is dangerous.

The man, skydiving right now, is scared.

The man is skydiving.

In the first sentence “skydiving” is the subject of the verb “is” and is therefore acting as a noun.  It is a gerund.  In the second sentence “skydiving” is a participle and is acting adjectivally to the noun “man”.  The third sentence is another example which can really throw people off.  In that sentence the verb in the sentence is “is skydiving” and is NOT acting as a gerund or a participle.  Here are some more examples in English of gerunds:

Eating people is wrong.

Hunting tigers is dangerous.

Flying makes me nervous.

Can you sneeze without opening your mouth?

She avoided him by walking on the opposite side of the road.

I look forward to hearing from you soon.

She didn't really take to studying English.

Luckily in Latin this isn’t as complicated.  Once you have learned how to form a gerund there will be NO overlap between participles or verbs.

Gerund as simple noun:

The Gerund functions in grammatical cases like any other noun; but it retains its verbal force and may control a direct object or be modified by an adverb:

Cupidus pugnandi est.

He is desirous of fighting. Objective. Gen.

Legendo discimus.

We learn by (means of) reading. Abl. of Means 

Regi libenter parendo, fortiores fiemus.*

By obeying the king willingly,  we shall become stronger. Abl. of Means

Gerund to express purpose:

Ad + the gerund

Causa + the gerund

Ad legendum venit.

He comes for the purpose of reading (to read).

Legendi causa venit.

He comes for the sake of reading (to read).

Gerundives

Gerundives are a mystery to many Latin students because they don’t exist in English. Well, they exist, but most English grammarians don’t recognize them.  The gerundive is also called the future passive participle.  The Gerundive is a verbal adjective and is always passive. It is formed by adding -ndus, -a, -um (-iendus, -a, -um with I-stems and 4th conjugation verbs) to the stem of the verb. It declines like a lst and 2nd declension adjective. These forms will indeed overlap with the gerund forms learned previously.  There is one clue to help you recognize which is which.  First of all, gerunds can only take 2nd declension singular neuter endings.  Gerundives can take ALL 1st, 2nd, and 2nd neuter endings.  These are adjective endings and gerundives are adjectives.

Gerundives can be used and translated in several different ways:

Gerundive as simple adjective:

The Gerundive carries a notion of necessity, obligation or propriety and can be used (like any adjective) in simple agreement with its noun:

Libros legendos habeo.

I have books to be read (that must be/should be read).

Fortis et laudandus vir est.

He is a brave man and to be praised.

Iniuria non ferenda facta est.

An injury not to be borne has been done.

Gerundive with Direct Object: 

The Gerundive is always preferred over the Gerund where a direct object in the accusative case is involved:

Libris legendis discimus.

We learn by books being read (by reading books).

(written in the more natural but incorrect way: Libros legendo discimus)

Cupidus librorum legendorum est.

He is desirous of books being read (of reading books).

(written in the more natural but incorrect way: Cupidus libros legendi est)

Gerundive with ad + acc. and gen. + causa to express purpose:

The Gerundive (like the Gerund) may be used with ad + acc. or gen. + causa to express purpose. This is the regular construction where a Direct Object is involved.

Librorum legendorum causa venit.

He comes for the sake of books being read (to read books).

Ad libros legendos venit.

He comes for the purpose of books being read (to read books)

The Passive Periphrastic Construction:

Finally, the gerundive is used with sum in the Future Passive Periphrastic Construction. Here, also, there is always a notion of necessity or obligation. The construction is passive; and the gerundive will agree (number, case, and gender) with the subject of the sentence. Since the gerundive here functions in a verbal setting, agreeing with the subject of the sentence, the case will always be nominative (or accusative in indirect speech).

Carthago delenda est.

Carthage must (should) be destroyed.

Nautae videndi sunt.

The sailors must be seen.

Dative of Agent with Passive Periphrastic:

If the agent of the action is expressed, the dative case is used

Carthago nobis delenda est.

Carthage must be destroyed by us.

Nautae videndi sunt tibi.

The sailors must be seen by you.

Conditions

Conditions are notoriously one of the most confusing parts of Latin grammar and have hounded the less than attentive students for many generations.  Let’s try to make learning this as simple as possible!

A condition is any sentence that contains the words “if” or “unless”? In Latin these are the words “si” and “nisi”.  These sentences are broken up into two halves.  The “if (si)” side is called the protasis.  The “then” side is called the apodosis.

protasis

apodosis

If I study….

I will do well in school.

If a shape has three sides...

it is a triangle.

If Caesar marches on Rome...

Pompey will not be happy.

The numbers represent the possible combinations of conditions.  For instance the “1” would be a present real condition.  The “2” would be a past real condition etc.

Let’s touch on each of these individually and then come back and fill in these numbers with some more information.   One simple note is that all “real” conditions will use indicative verbs, and all “unreal” conditions will use subjunctive verbs.

  1. Present Real (Present simple fact)

The protasis and the apodosis will use present indicative verbs.  This sentence will show a real and present fact.

Si in foro ambulo, Cicero me videt.

If I am walking in the forum, Cicero sees me.

2. Past Real (Past simple fact)

The protasis and the apodosis will use past tense indicative verbs.  This sentence will show a real and past fact.

Si in foro ambulabam, Cicero me vidit.

If I was walking in forum, Cicero saw me.

3. Future Real (Future more vivid)

The protasis and the apodosis will use future indicative verbs.  This sentence will present a real and present fact.

Si in foro ambulabo, Cicero me videbit.

If I walk in the forum, Cicero will see me.

This one is a little extra tricky.  In the protasis English uses a present tense verb even though it should really be future.  Think about it…..in the sentence above what I am really saying is “If I WILL walk in the forum, Cicero will see me.”  Again, English uses the present tense in this instance which doesn’t represent reality as well as the Latin does.

4. Present Unreal (Present contrary to fact)

The protasis and the apodosis will use imperfect subjunctive verbs.  This sentence will show something that is taking place right now but isn’t true.  This is one of the first sets of conditions that may not seem to make much sense.  We are using an imperfect tense verb to express something happening in the present!!

Si in foro ambularem, Cicero me videret.

If I were walking in the forum (right now), Cicero would see me.

5. Past Unreal (Past contrary to fact)

The protasis and the apodosis will use pluperfect subjunctive verbs.  This sentence will show past tense situation that did not occur.  Latin doesn’t do a good job representing the actual tenses here even though it is the correct way to do it.

Si in foro ambulavissem, Cicero me vidisset.

If I had walked in the forum, Cicero would have seen me.

6. Future Unreal (Future contrary to fact)

The protasis and the apodosis will use present subjunctive verbs (remember, there are no future subjunctive verb forms!).  This sentence will show an event that would take place in the future (if it were to happen, but probably wouldn’t).  This type of condition is often called the “should-would” condition.  The “should” half has fallen into disuse, however, and normally is “would” is used instead.

Si in foro ambulem, Cicero me videat.

If I would walk in the forum, Cicero would see me.
