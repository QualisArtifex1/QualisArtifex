Imperatives

So far the only verb forms that have been introduced in the textbook are in the present tense.  Now you are going to learn how to give direct commands.  These forms are more likely to appear in works of literature with direct speech and dialogue like plays and epic poetry.

An imperative is used when you want to give a command. Technically the imperative is a type of MOOD of a verb.  The mood of all previous verbs learned so far, the ones used in a simple sentence is called the INDICATIVE.

This verb form is actually quite easy.  All you need to do is find the stem of a verb by chopping the “-re” off the infinitive (2nd principle part)....That’s it!

So,

Amare - “re” = ama

Ama = love!

“Ama” is the imperative form of the verb amo, amare and it traditionally ended with an exclamation mark to show that it is a command in English.  Now this rule ONLY applies if you are speaking to one person.  If you want to address a group of people you need to make the imperative plural.  This is also quite easy.  For most instances all you need to do is add a “-te” to the end of the singular form. So…

Ama = Love! (to one person)

Ama + te = amate = Love! (to a group)

As with all rules in languages, there are a few exceptions.  Let’s take a look at them.

Negative Imperatives

What if you want to tell someone to NOT do something?  What changes?  Fortunately there is an easy solution.  Anytime you want to give a NEGATIVE command all you need to do is use the imperative form of the verb “to not wish” (noli/nolite) and then add an infinitive after it.  Literally what you are saying is, “Do not wish” to do something.  Let’s use the verbs from the first chart to show how this works.

Vocative Case

The vocative case is what I would consider an extra noun case.  It doesn’t fit into the charts and tables that have been provided in previous chapters.  Many British schoolboys were made to memorize it along with the rest of the cases.  I find that the rule is so simple and it doesn’t vary enough that it is far easier just to remember one rule.  

The vocative case is the case that is used for nouns when you are addressing it.  For instance, in English, if I said:

Michael, clean your room!

Michael would be in the vocative case.  

So, what does this look like in Latin?  Simple.  In most instances the vocative case looks identical to the nominative case.

Vocative = Nominative

The two exceptions to this rule are when a noun ends in “-us” or “-ius”,  When either of these occur the following will happen.

-us -----> e

-ius -----> i      

So, almost all male names that fall into the 2nd declension (which is a lot), will fall into one of those two categories.  Luckily the Romans gave us a very famous Roman, by the name of Marcus Tullius Cicero, a famous orator and senator, whose three names meets the rule and BOTH exceptions!  It is great for illustrating this topic.  If i wanted to address Cicero, how would I do so?  What would his name look like?

Let’s try looking at one of your basic sentences that has both a vocative noun and an imperative verb in order to review both topics.  For text color purposes, I will use a DARK RED for the vocative case because it so close to the nominative.

7.1 'O socii (neque enim ignari sumus ante malorum)…

                                     

Revocate animos, maestumque timorem mittite.’

Notice the word “socii” is vocative.  It looks identical to the nominative plural.  It is paired with the two imperative verbs in the second line.  Notice how those verbs are both PLURAL imperative because Aeneas is addressing his “socii”, a group of people.  Notice also the clever word order in the second line.  By ending and beginning the line with a command Vergil has created an ABBA word order.  This is called CHIASMUS and is considered to be beautiful in poetry and prose alike.  (Ask not what your country can do for you, but what you can do for your country)

O comrades (for we are not ignorant of bad things previously), recall (your) spirits and send away (your) sad fear.

(Marcus Tullius Cicero)

Concessive Clause

This is just an introduction to concessive clauses because later on they will get a bit more complicated.  A concessive clause is a clause that is conceding something.  The most common word that does this in English is “although”.  So, the best way to see this for the first time in Latin is by learning the word “quamquam” which means although.  Many times when you see the word “quamquam” used in a sentence you will later see the word “tamen” which means “nevertheless”.  A sentence might look like this:

[Quamquam puer puellam amat], tamen puella puerum non amat

Although the boy loves the girl, nevertheless the girl does not love the boy.

Since, “quamquam” is a subordinate conjunction and starts a clause, it should be marked by brackets when you are annotating your text.

Basic Sentence 7.2 has a good example of this.
