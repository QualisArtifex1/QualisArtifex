Present Subjunctive

In Latin, the verb forms you have learned in all previous chapters have all been (except for the imperative commands) part of the indicative mood.  Four of the six verb tenses that you have learned will have a mirror image in the subjunctive mood.  Why have a different mood at all?  Well, in Latin, as in English, the mood of a verb is used for very specific purposes.

In addition to the various tenses, verbs can exist in three moods:

  • indicative--for stating facts
  • subjunctive--for stating possibilities, conjectures, "what if," what someone else said, thought or believed, polite commands, and several other uses.
  • imperative--for stating commands

This chapter introduces the present tense of the subjunctive mood.  Before we learn the specific uses of the subjunctive mood, let’s learn what the new forms are.

To make a present tense INDICATIVE verb, into the SUBJUNCTIVE mood, one merely has to change the internal vowel of the verb.  The new vowels that take the place of the old vowels can easily be remembered by the mnemonic device;

“We Fear A Liar, Friar”

Each of the words above correspond to a conjugation and the underlined letter(s) will replace the internal vowel of the indicative verb.  Let’s take a look at a chart to have it make a little more sense.

Independent Uses of the Subjunctive

The subjunctive mood can be used in many different ways, some of which will be introduced later in this chapter.  In this section we will be looking at the way the subjunctive can be used in INDEPENDENT clauses.  These subjunctives are all by themselves out in the open.  These occur less often than the dependent uses which you will learn later.

A. Jussive and Hortatory Subjunctive:

The Jussive (iubeo, -ere, iussi, iussus) and Hortatory (hortor, -ari, -atus sum) are used to express a command or an exhortation.  Consider Basic Sentence 15.1 in addition to the following words:

Veniat   !             Let him come!         (Jussive)

amemus!             Let us love!         (Hortatory)

The Jussive occurs in the third person, the Hortatory in the first.

The negative is introduced by ne:

Ne hoc faciat!    Let him not do this!

B. Potential Subjunctive:

The subjunctive may be used to express an action which might possibly occur:

Haec credas.                                        You might believe these things.

Dicas eum hominem bonum esse.        You might say he is a good man.

C. Deliberative Subjunctive: 

The subjunctive is used to deliberate about something, often in a rhetorical question:

Quid faciam?                                       What am I to do?

The negative is introduced by non.

 

D. Optative Subjunctive: 

The Optative (opto -are) is used for a future wish capable of fulfillment, introduced by utinam or ut (negative utinam ne or ne).  Utinam can best be read as “would that” which sounds a bit old-fashioned.  A more modern translation is “if only”:

Utinam veniat!                                     If only he would come!

Utinam ne veniat!                                If only he would not come!

Of all of these uses, the most common is the hortatory subjunctive, but even this use isn’t even close in frequency to the dependent uses which will be learned soon.

Purpose Clause

The purpose clause is likely the most common use of the subjunctive mood.  It can also be a little tricky because English doesn’t often express this type of clause in the same way.  

In English, if I want to express the purpose behind an action, there are two main ways of doing this.  The first and more common way is to use an infinitive.  Take the following example:

I went to the store to buy groceries.

In this sentence the infinitive verb “to buy” is used to express the purpose behind the main action (going to the store).  The other way to express this type of action is to use the subordinating conjunction “so that”.  Consider the following:

I went to the store so that I could buy groceries.

You might notice that these two sentences are saying basically the same thing.  The way Latin will express this type of sentence is to use the very important subordinating conjunction “ut” or “ne” in the negative and THEN the verb inside of that clause will be in the subjunctive mood.

Purpose clauses act as an adverb clause; that is, they may modify the main verb by indicating "how" someone acted, under what terms or provisions. Purpose Clauses, then, are just ways of drawing the actor's will or intention (in the form of an indirect command) into the sentence as an adverbial modifier of the action. There are lots of other ways to express purpose, but this is the most common and handy.

UT in Purpose clauses = So that/In order to

Let’s take a look at Basic Sentence 15.5 to see an example.

                                               

Hanc Graecis conscriptam litteris mittit, [ne intercepta epistola nostra ab

                         

hostibus consilia cognoscantur.]

****************************************************************

(He) sent this (letter) having been written in Greek letters, so that with

that letter having been intercepted, our plans are not learned by the

enemies.

Notice that there are a number of words in the translation that don’t have a 1-1 analogue.  The subject here is missing in the Latin (he).  “Ne” in the Latin corresponds to the words “so that...not” in the English. You can also see that the verb “cognoscantur” is in the subjunctive mood.  This is because it is the main verb in the purpose clause.  This will ALWAYS be the case.  The verb is 3rd conjugation and has replaced its normal internal vowel with an “a”.

Indirect Command

An indirect command is very similar to a purpose clause with one major difference.  This is that it requires a verb with SPECIFIC semantic meanings to set up the clause. In addition to this, indirect commands are most easily translated into English with an infinitive.  Below are some Latin verbs to look out for that can set up an indirect command.  This list in NOT exhaustive, but it is a good starting point.

VERBS OF ASKING, ORDERING, BEGGING, DEMANDING,PERSUADING, OR WARNING:

 

Moneo, monere

Oro, orare

Rogo, are

Persaudeo, ere

Hortor, ari

Impero, are

Admoneo, ere

Peto, ere

*Remembered as MORPHIA P

The one BIG exception to this is the verb Iubeo, ere.  This verb DOES NOT use an indirect command.  It just uses an infinitive, much like English does.

Once you have spotted UT or NE, it is now more important to look at the Main Clause. If you have one of the MORPHIA  P verbs, you must be careful not to fall automatically into translating UT or NE with all the various possibilities available in a Purpose Clause.  In an Indirect Command, it is usually unnecessary (indeed, wrong most of the time) to try to say "so that" or "in order to".

Most usually, you should just translate UT as "to…", and NE as "not to…."

           e.g.   NOS  ROGAT  UT  MANEAMUS

    means   "He is asking us TO stay"

         not    "He is asking us SO THAT we may stay"!

On the whole, though, it is much more likely that you will find using a straight Infinitive in English gives you the translation you need.

Let’s take a look at Basic Sentence 15.4 to see a more authentic example:

Consurgitur ex consilio; comprehendunt utrumque et orant, [ne sua

dissensione et pertinacia rem in summum periculum deducant.]

****************************************************************

It was arisen (they arose) from the meeting; they embraced each other 

and begged [to not lead (this) situation into the greatest dangers because

of their own disagreement and defiance.

A couple of things to notice about this sentence.  The “ne” is being translated as “to not”.  It could also in this instance be translated as just “that”.  We also have one of the MORPHIA P words (oro) to indicate that the following clause is indeed an indirect command.  The verb “deducant” INSIDE of the indirect command is in the subjunctive mood as it MUST be inside of ALL indirect commands.  Also note the word “utrumque” (one of your vocabulary words this chapter) is accusative. The “-que” is a suffix.

UT in Indirect Command = that/to
