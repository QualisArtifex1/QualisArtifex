Pluperfect Subjunctive

A quick note about the order of presentation.  Normally the perfect subjunctive would have been introduced in this chapter as opposed to the pluperfect subjunctive.  The reasoning behind this switch is because the pluperfect subjunctive is far more common in general, and is used in the clauses in this chapter far more frequently.  This allowed me to throw a greater net to gather examples for clauses that began with the word “cum”.

This is the 3rd tense of the subjunctive that will be introduced.  As in the last chapter, do not forget that the pluperfect tense was already introduced in a previous chapter, but those rules only apply for the indicative mood.  There are NEW rules for the subjunctive mood for the same tense.  This new mood/tense combo will only be used in clauses that require subjunctive mood verbs but the translations of these verbs will not be any different than their indicative cousins.

Remember when you learned the perfect/pluperfect/future perfect tense verbs previously?  One thing you may have forgotten is that when you make any of these verb tenses passive, it will be made up of two words (paraphrastic).  This means that the rules you will learn will be two-fold: one for the active and one for the passive.

PLUPERFECT ACTIVE SUBJUNCTIVE

3rd principal part + sse + person ending

Ex: amavi+sse+t

amavisset

This tense is generally easy to recognize from its distinctive double “ss”.

For the passive system of the pluperfect subjunctive, we need to start with the 4th principal and then, as a second word, a form of the verb “sum, esse”.

PLUPERFECT PASSIVE SUBJUNCTIVE

4th principal part + imperfect subjunctive form of the verb “sum,esse”

Ex: amatus esset

It may seem odd that the imperfect subjunctive form of “sum, esse” is being used in a pluperfect verb.  This is however true.  One way to help you remember this is that you see this infamous double “ss” again.  When you see “ss” in a verb, the pluperfect subjunctive is probably your best bet!  Another reminder: don’t forget that in all verbs that have two words, the first word has to match the subject in case, number, and gender.  The chart above therefore does not contain all possible forms.

Cum as a Subordinating Conjunction

In early Latin, the Romans used a conjunction “quom”. This conjunction had various meanings, dependent upon the mood of the verb in its clause, and sometimes dependent upon the context in which the sentence occurred. Unfortunately, this conjunction “quom” came to be spelled “cum” by Caesar’s day. It MUST NOT be confused with the preposition cum, used with the ablative. Word order rules must be observed to distinguish among these words.

Four different subordinate clauses may begin with the conjunction “cum”. The first two types are called Cum Temporal Clauses and Cum Circumstantial Clauses.

1. Cum Temporal Clauses

One of the first clauses you learned in this textbook was a temporal clause, using the word “ubi”.  A temporal clause beginning with “cum” must contain an indicative verb. This indicative verb names a specific action which allows its clause to answer the question, “When did the main clause verb happen?” Any “cum” clause which contains an indicative verb is a temporal clause.

[Cum Caesar occisus est,] multi Romani tristissimi erant.

When Caesar was killed, many Romans were very mournful.

[When were many Romans very sad? At the time Caesar was killed.]

The Basic Sentence that correlates to this is:

17.3  Ac veluti magno in populo cum saepe coorta est

         seditio, saevitque animis ignobile volgus,

         iamque faces et saxa volant—furor arma ministrat;              

Notice that the verbs used are INDICATIVE (underlined above) and therefore the clause MUST be temporal and “cum” translated as “when”.

2. Cum Circumstantial Clauses

When a general set of circumstances is referred to, rather than a time-specific action, a subjunctive verb is used following the conjunction “cum”. This is because general circumstances were viewed as more vague by the Romans. “Cum” is still translated as when. This may seem confusing.  Why have two clauses that both use the same conjunction, both are translated as “when”, but only one uses subjunctive verbs?!  It is all about how vague the scenario is.  The more general the scenario, the more likely the clause is to use the subjunctive.

[Cum Caesar praefectus in Gallia esset,] erat pax.

When Caesar was governor in Gaul, there was peace.

[When was there peace? Under the circumstances that Caesar was governor in Gaul. (9 whole years!)]

Specific = “cum” Temporal Clause

That night [when they arrived at Rome,] they talked with the leader.

Vague (whenever) = “cum” Circumstantial Clause

I advise my friend [when I see him.]

The Basic Sentence that correlates to this is:

17.2 Cum civitas ob eam rem incitata armis ius suum exsequi conaretur multitudinemque hominum ex agris magistratus cogerent, Orgetorix mortuus est.

3. Cum Causal Clauses

Several conjunctions can begin Causal Clauses in Latin. The one you have been used to seeing is the causal clause starting with the conjunction “quod”.  That type of clause ALWAYS uses an indicative verb.  A Causal Clause answers the question, “Why was the main clause verb done?”   Some causal clauses are less authoritative, and they contain subjunctive verbs. Theoretically, you will be able to tell how secure Caesar is with his facts, based upon what conjunction he has used to begin his subordinate clause! “Cum” can indeed start a causal clause and in this instance it will mean “because/since”.

“Cum”, when it means “because” or “since,” always is followed by a subjunctive verb.

Dux urbem dedidit [cum hostes extra portas essent.]

The general surrendered the city [since the enemy were outside the gates.]

The Basic Sentence that correlates to this is:

17.4  Cum celeriter nostri arma cepissent vallumque adscendissent atque una ex parte Hispanis equitibus emissis equestri proelio superiores fuissent, desperata re hostes suos ab oppugnatione reduxerunt.

Whether or not a Latin Causal Clause contains a subjunctive mood verb, always translate the verb as though it were indicative. “Cum” Causal Clauses are often difficult to identify in Latin, because they may share the same formula with “cum” Circumstantial and “cum” Concessive Clauses. The best guide to determine whether a “cum” clause is causal is context.

4. Cum Concessive Clauses

Concessive Clauses always begin with “although” or “even if,” and they represent the first part of a contradictory statement. The subordinating conjunction “although” (“cum”) is an attempt to reconcile the contradiction. Sometimes, the coordinating conjunction “nevertheless” balances the “although.”

The sun is shining.            It is raining.

Although the sun is shining, it is nevertheless raining.

“Cum” Concessive Clauses can be very difficult to distinguish from “cum” Circumstantial and “cum” Causal Clauses, because they may share the same formula. If you are fortunate, a “cum” Concessive Clause will be followed by a main clause containing “tamen” (nevertheless). If you are not fortunate, then you will need to look for whether the two clauses represent an apparent contradiction just as in the Basic Sentence that demonstrates this type of clause:

 17.1 At tanta militum virtus atque ea praesentia animi fuit, ut, cum undique flamma torrerentur maximaque telorum multitudine premerentur suaque omnia impedimenta atque omnes fortunas conflagrare intellegerent, non modo demigrandi causa de vallo decederet nemo, sed paene ne respiceret quidem quisquam…
