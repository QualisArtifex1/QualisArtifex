3rd Declension

Hopefully you have taken the time to memorize the 1st and 2nd declension chart by now, because you are now going to be given even more forms!  

Just as some nouns fall into either 1st or 2nd or 2nd neuter declensions, some nouns take a totally different pattern from what you’ve learned so far.  This is the 3rd declension.  It’s pattern of endings has some similarities to what you have seen before but for the most part is quite different.  Similar to 2nd declension, 3rd declension comes in a masculine/feminine (same endings) and a neuter variety.  I would note for you that instead of internalizing neuter declension endings (2nd or 3rd) it might be best just to learn to rule for neuter endings and apply it to whatever declension you are working with.  

Neuter Declension Rules

  1. The nominative and the accusative will be the same
  2. The nominative and accusative plural will end in “a”

One thing to note about the 3rd declension is that masculine and feminine 3rd declension nouns have the same endings.  In fact, in many cases it is impossible to know the gender of a 3rd declension noun unless you know the dictionary entry or if there is an adjective describing the noun.  The 3rd declension chart is below.  The 1st and 2nd declension chart is here too so you can compare the endings side by side.

3rd declension nouns will also look a little different when you see them in a dictionary entry, though they will follow the same rules

The issue that may throw you off is that the nominative of the 3rd declension can end in a variety of ways and in fact in the chart above is just marked with a “*”.  This is because the 3rd declension nominative has no regular form.  This is why it is so important that it is listed in the dictionary entry.  Otherwise there would be no way to derive that form!  One more important thing to note is that to find the stem of a 3rd declension noun you must use the genitive form and remove the “is”.  This stem is what you will add all your endings to.  Let’s take a quick look at rex, regis to illustrate this.

Masc/Fem

You can see in the nominative there is an “x” on the end of the word.  This “x” is not repeated throughout the ending because the stem of the word is take from the genitive, “regis”.  You can remove the ending “is” and add all future endings to the stem “reg”.

a.) "Parisyllabic": If the nominative ends in -is or -es, and the nominative and genitive have the same number of syllables, then the noun is an i-stem. civis, civis, m/f

My own personal belief is that this does NOT need to be memorized, though some teachers would disagree with me.  Here is why:  something very important to understand about i-stems that is rarely ever mentioned is why they are called "i-stem". It is not because of a set of rules that these nouns use special endings (though those rules are useful in practice); i-stem nouns use the same 3rd declension endings as all other 3rd declension nouns, it is just that their stem ends in a vowel rather than a consonant, causing certain anomalies.

For example, a "normal" 3rd declension noun such as rex, called a consonant stem for the final letter of its stem, reg-. To the stem you add the regular endings -s, -is, -i, -em, -e, -es, -um, -ibus, -es, -ibus.
Typical reasoning says that with an i-stem like ignis, the stem is ign-. To that stem you add different endings than you would to a non i-stem: -is, -is, -i, -em, -i, -es, -ium, -ibus, -es (-is), -ibus. In reality, the stem is igni-, hence its name as an i-stem; when you combine igni- and -um you get the genitive plural ignium, no different from combining reg- and -um to get regum. You can add -s to igni- or reg- to get the nominative singular, so once again i-stems do not differ from consonant stems. In the same way, you can think of maria as being mari + a instead of the less accurate mar + ia.

I believe that thinking of i-stems in this way, as having the same endings as consonant stems, is a much better approach and more appropriate than regarding them as irregular.

Adjectives in 3rd Declension

This topic on the surface seems to be kind of an easy one.  Some adjectives, just like nouns, can fall into 3rd declension. There. Wasn’t that easy?  However, I find that students often struggle with this concept and you’ll see why as it is explained.  

For the most part, before the chapter, the great majority of times you have seen an adjective describe a noun, the endings would look the same.  For instance:

Puella magna

Animus bonus

Monstrum malum

Adjectives will ALWAYS match their nouns in case, number, and gender.  When they look alike too, that makes things a lot easier.  Unfortunately now the landscape for adjectives has become much more diverse.

Let’s take a look at an adjective from this chapter’s vocabulary to dive a little deeper.

Fortis, forte

This adjective’s dictionary entry probably looks drastically different from the adjective entries you saw for 1st/2nd declension.  Here’s an example of a 1st/2nd declension adjective dictionary entry:

Bonus, a, um

If you recall, the forms given in an adjective dictionary entry are the nominative masculine, feminine, and neuter forms.  This is in contrast to the nominative, genitive entry for a noun.  For 3rd declension adjectives this entry is no different from its 1st/2nd declension cousin.  The only difference is that in 3rd declension, the masculine and feminine forms are the same (as seen in the noun chart from earlier this chapter), so the first form is both the masculine AND the feminine form.

Fortis, forte = masculine/feminine, neuter

N.B.  ALL 3rd declension adjectives are i-stem and in fact the ablative singular will always be an “i” except sometimes in poetry.

Less frequently some 3rd declension adjectives have an odd masculine nominative singular form and will give you three forms in the dictionary entry, like the word:

You’ll notice that without the form “acer” in that entry it would otherwise be identical to fortis, forte.  Adjectives like “acer” are called 3rd declension adjectives of 3 terminations.

Even more rarely, 3rd declension adjectives can have the same forms for all three genders.  Adjectives like this are called 3rd declension adjective of 1 termination.

Now comes the fun part!

If a noun and adjective live in the same declension, everything becomes relatively easy because the endings will generally look alike. Here are some examples:

Regis tristis = sad king (genitive)

Puella bona = good girl (nominative)

Militibus fortibus = brave soldiers (ablative or dative)

But what happens if a noun lives in 1st declension and an adjective lives in 3rd!? Or vice versa?! Well, the adjective will still match in case, number and gender, but the endings will not look alike.  Here are some examples:

Puella fortis = brave girl (nominative)

Rex bonus  = good king (nominative)

Present Active Participle (PAP)

Back to verbs.  You remember those, right?  Well, this is the perfect place to introduce the present active participle.  First it would be wise to define what a participle is in English.  There are several different kinds of participles, but we are only going to discuss the present active variety for now, from now on to be referred to as the PAP.

A participle is a verbal adjective.  What that means is that you start with a verb and apply adjectival qualities to it.  In English, PAPs are easy to recognize by the “-ing” (though not all words that end in “-ing” are PAPs).  Here is an example:

Caesar, destroying the city, ended the war.

The main sentence pieces here are Caesar (subject), ended (verb) and war (direct object).  So we could just start with this:

Caesar ended the war.

Now let’s look at the last two words.  Destroying is a PAP.  it comes from the verb “to destroy” and has an “-ing” attached at the end.  Though this word was born as a verb, it has been converted to an adjective.  “Destroying” is describing the noun “Caesar”.  Who is destroying something?  Caesar is.    So what do I do with the word “city”?  This word is actually a direct object of the PAP “destroying”!  That’s right!  Because “destroying” originally came from a verb it never really lost its ability to take a direct object.

    

Caesar, destroying the city, ended the war.  

There are 2 direct objects here.  One goes with the verb and one with the PAP.  If this were written in Latin, “destroying” would have to be in the same case, number, and gender as “Caesar”.

Let’s talk about how to form a PAP in Latin.  The reason why this subject was placed into this chapter was because PAPs will take 3rd declension endings.  The nominative is formed by taking the stem of the verb and adding “ns”.  The genitive is formed by adding an “nt” to the stem and then adding your normal 3rd declension endings.

  1. Participles are verbal adjectives
  2. Shows an action happening at the same time as the main verb
  3. Identify with an “-ns” or “-nt-” + 3RD DECLENSION ENDING
  4. Translate with “-ing”

Basic Sentence 6.2 has a good example.

 

Gens inimica mihi Tyrrhenum navigat aequor, [Ilium in Italiam portans…]

This sentence has already been dissected previously, so let’s focus on just the part after the comma.  “Portans” is a PAP which comes from the verb “porto” which means “to carry”. So, a PAP would be translated as “carrying”.  Who is carrying something?  Well, if you look at the case, it is nominative and is therefore describing “gens”.   You can see too that I have placed brackets around some of the words.  When a participle evokes objects or other words to appear nearby this is called a participial phrase.

A race, unfriendly to me, sails the Tyrrhenian sea, [ carrying Troy into Italy.]

It’s easy once you get the hang of it!  Let’s take one last look at an example of a PAP from each conjugation of verbs:

Amo, amare, amavi, amatum = amans, amantis = loving

Video, videre, vidi, visum = videns, videntis = seeing

Rego, regere, rexi, rectum = regens, regentis = ruling

Venio, venire, veni, ventum = veniens, venientis = arriving (notice the “ie”)

Questions, Numbers, and Dates

Questions

Romans didn’t use question marks, so how do we know when a question is being asked?  The types of questions that are the hardest to express without a question mark are “yes or no” questions.  One of your vocab words for the chapter, “-ne” is not actually even a word.  It is what is called an “enclitic”, which is just a fancy term for a chunk of letters attached to a word.  It is kind of like a suffix.  This suffix has no meaning at all other than to indicate that the sentence is a question.  “-ne” is always attached to the first word of the sentence.

Puella puerum amat. = The girl loves the boy.

Puellane puerum amat? = Does the girl love the boy?

There are other words in Latin that also indicate a “yes or no” question, but they have additional meaning.  “Nonne” as the first word of a sentence will indicate a “yes or no” question with the expectation of the answer as “yes”.  

Nonne puella puerum amat? = Surely the girl loves the boy/The girl loves the boy, right?

The word “num” does the same thing as “nonne” except it expects the answer as “no”.

Num puella puerum amat? = The girl doesn’t love the boy, does she?

Dates

The Roman dating system for dating is rather complex.  In this textbook there isn’t sufficient space to go into all the details of the Julian Calendar and the cultural reasons for the names we’ll see.  Here are the basics.

For years, the Romans either used the date 753 B.C. as the “founding of the city” and all years would be counted from that date and abbreviated A.U.C. (Ab urbe condita = from the founding of the city).  The other way years could be described once the Republic began ( 509 B.C.) is by saying which two consuls were leading during that year.  All Roman consuls served for one year only and there were always two consuls.  Consuls could also not run again for 10 years once they served so in this way you could always tell what year it was by who the consuls were.  By putting the word “consulibus” and both the names of the consuls in the ablative case (NIAWOPs) this would give a year (at least if you know your history it does).  Look at Basic Sentence 6.3.

6.3 Is dies est a. d. V. Kal. Apr. L. Pisone, A. Gabinio consulibus.

During the consulship of Lucius Piso and Aulus Gabinius (58 B.C.)

For the day of the month it is even more complex.  Specific dates were not given.  Each day of the month was measured by how many days until the next important day in the calendar.  Sometimes this meant referencing a date in the NEXT month to give a date THIS month.  Each month had exactly three days that it reckoned from.  All Roman dates count INCLUSIVELY also, meaning that you count the day you are counting FROM and the day you are counting TO.  

The Kalends = 1st

The Nones = 9 days before Ides. The 5th (7th in March, October, May, and July)

The Ides = The 13th (15th in March, October, May, and July)

Let’s check out our Basic Sentence again:

6.3 Is dies est a. d. V. Kal. Apr. L. Pisone, A. Gabinio consulibus.

A.d. here stands for “ante dies” (days before).  The “V” is the Roman numeral for 5.   Kal. Apr. is short for the Kalends of April.  So, 5 days before the Kalends of April INCLUDING April 1st and moving BACKWARDS is March 28th.

Let’s try one more to practice:

A.d. IV Id. Oct. = 4 days before the Ides of October.  The Ides of October is the 15th.  4 days before that is October 12th. (Remember to count INCLUSIVELY!)
