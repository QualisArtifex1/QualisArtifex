Perfect Subjunctive

The last form of the subjunctive is the perfect subjunctive.  There are no more verb tenses to learn in all of Latin!  Let’s get to it.  The perfect subjunctive is the rarest of the subjunctive verb tenses. It is also one of the most confusing because many of its forms look almost identical to the future perfect indicative.  The following two paragraphs are the same as in Chapter 17, but it bears repeating because of its importance.

 As in the last chapter, do not forget that the perfect tense was already introduced in a previous chapter, but those rules only apply for the indicative mood.  There are NEW rules for the subjunctive mood for the same tense.  This new mood/tense combo will only be used in clauses that require subjunctive mood verbs but the translations of these verbs will not be any different than their indicative cousins.

Remember when you learned the perfect/pluperfect/future perfect tense verbs previously?  One thing you may have forgotten is that when you make any of these verb tenses passive, it will be made up of two words (paraphrastic).  This means that the rules you will learn will be two-fold: one for the active and one for the passive.

PERFECT ACTIVE SUBJUNCTIVE

3rd principal part (minus -i) + eri + person ending

Ex: amav+eri+t

amaverit

The active forms are identical to the future perfect indicative except for the 1st person singular.

* The future perfect indicative is “amavero”.  The subjunctive mood always uses “m” as its 1st person singular active person ending.

For the passive system of the pluperfect subjunctive, we need to start with the 4th principal and then, as a second word, a form of the verb “sum, esse”.

PERFECT PASSIVE SUBJUNCTIVE

4th principal part + Subjunctive form of “sum, esse”

Ex: amatus sit

Perfect Passive Subjunctive example #1

singular

plural

1st person

Amati simus

2nd person

3rd person

Amati sint

Compare these forms to the perfect passive indicative.  Amatus est

Amatus est (indicative) --------------> amatus sit (subjunctive)

Flipping that second word to the subjunctive makes the whole phrase (paraphrastic) a subjunctive verb.  These verbs will never be translated any differently than its indicative cousin.  It just has to be subjunctive because certain clauses require it to be.  This is the only reason.

Sequence of Tenses

Now that you know all of the subjunctive verb tenses we need to learn when to use which tense.  There are luckily a few simple rules.  These rules are called the sequence of tenses.  In order to learn the rules below you need to know when a verb is considered to be primary and when a verb is considered to be secondary.

Primary

Present

Future

Future perfect

Secondary

Perfect*

Imperfect

Pluperfect

*The perfect indicative is ordinarily a secondary tense, but allows the primary sequence when the present time is clearly in the writer's mind.

In applying the rule for the Sequence of Tenses, observe—

  1. Whether the main verb is (a) primary or (b) secondary.

  1. Whether the dependent verb is to denote completed action (i.e. past with reference to the main verb) or incomplete action (i.e. present or future with reference to the main verb).

a. If the leading verb is primary, the dependent verb must be in the present subjunctive if it denotes something happening at the same time as the leading verb, in the perfect subjunctive if it denotes something happening at a time before the leading verb.

b. If the leading verb is secondary, the dependent verb must be in the imperfect subjunctive if it denotes something happening at the same time as the leading verb, in the pluperfect subjunctive if it denotes something happening at a time before the leading verb.

The primary verb is the verb of the main clause.  The dependent verb is the verb inside of the dependent clause (purpose, result, etc.)  The chart below distills the paragraphs above into a neat tidy package.  The columns will indicate which subjunctive verb needs to be used based on where the main verb is primary or secondary and whether the dependent verb is happening compared to the main verb.

Indirect Questions

What Are Indirect Questions?

In English, direct questions are the “normal” questions that we can ask friends, family members, and people whom we know well. They end in question marks.  You can form direct questions by using a question word (who, what, when etc.).  Sometimes in English just adding a question mark can make a sentence a question.  

Statement: The new baby is a boy.

Question: The new baby is a boy? (really?)

People use indirect questions when they’re asking for help in the street, because they are very polite. Indirect questions start with a phrase like 'could you tell me...' or 'do you know...'. For example:

Direct question: Where is the bank?

Indirect question: Could you tell me where the bank is?

Notice that in the indirect question I put the verb ('is') after the subject ('the bank'), but in the direct question I put the verb 'is' before the subject 'the bank'.  This is a good marker to know if you have an indirect question in English.

In Latin the main verb is a verb of mental action such as knowing, asking, seeing et al. and typically will be in the indicative mood, but it is not uncommon to see the main verb in the subjunctive mood as well.

  • The main verb is followed by an interrogative word or expression, such as the interrogative relative quis, quid, or others such as ubi, quomodo, quam, ut, et al.
  • The indirect question itself is in a dependent clause whose verb is always in the subjunctive mood.

Indirect Questions

Question word + Subjunctive Verb

The verb in an indirect question is translated with an indicative verb in English: there is no need to put should, may, or might into the translation for the subjunctive.

Nescio quid consul sit. = “I do not know what a consul is.” [NOT: “I do not know what a consul may be.”]

******************************************************************

Tenses in Indirect Questions: Indirect questions as with ALL dependent clauses in Latin use the sequence of tense rules.  Consider the following examples.

 ACTIVE VOICE EXAMPLES

Primary sequence:

Video quid laudes. = I see what you are praising.

Video quid laudaveris. = I see what you praised.

Secondary sequence:

Vidi quid laudares. = I saw what you were praising.

Vidi quid laudavisses. = I saw what you had praised.

PASSIVE VOICE EXAMPLES 

Primary sequence:

Video quid laudetur. = I see what is being praised.

Video quid laudatum sit. = I see what was praised.

Secondary sequence:

Vidi quid laudaretur. = I saw what was being praised.

Vidi quid laudatum esset. = I saw what had been praised.

Consider Basic Sentence 18.2

Interim speculatoribus in omnes partes dimissis explorat [quo commodissime itinere vallem transire possit.]

Meanwhile with scouts having been sent out into many locations, he explored [by what path he was able to cross the valley].

Note that the main verb (explorat) is present tense, HOWEVER, Caesar often uses the present tense when he means the past tense to show immediacy which is why we are translating it as past tense.  Notice how it follows sequence of tenses.

“Explorat” (primary sequence)---------------------> “possit” (same time).
