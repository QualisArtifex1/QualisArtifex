Supines

The supine is a verbal noun much like a gerund.  The difficulty for most students is that supines do not exist in English so there is nothing to compare it to.  In English, where Latin would use a supine, English uses a plain present active infinitive.

Supines are formed by using the 4th principal part of a verb and using 4th declension neuter noun forms.  In addition to this the supine ONLY uses the -um accusative form and the -u ablative form.  The supine is always translated as “to verb”

Supines

4th principal part with -um (accusative supine)

4th principal part with -u (ablative supine)

Amatum = to love

Amatu = to love

 

I. The Supine in -um:

The Supine in -um may be used after verbs of motion to express purpose and is more often used in prose over poetry:

Legatos ad Caesarem mittunt rogatum auxilium.

They send envoys to Caesar to ask for help.

Stultitia est venatum ducere invitas canes.

 It is folly to take unwilling dogs to hunt.

The construction is found most commonly with the verbs “ire” and “venire”.  Supine in -um may take a direct object, though the construction was not very common:

Hannibal patriam defensum revocatus est.

Hannibal was recalled to defend his country.

The Supine in -um, very common in early Latin, was not favored by Caesar. It was frequent again in Sallust and Livy but in Later Latin was generally supplanted by the infinitive of purpose, which is one of the ways that English expresses purpose.

Consider Basic Sentence 20.2

Indutiomari Treveri nuntiis impulsi suos concitaverunt subitoque oppressis lignatoribus magna manu ad castra oppugnatum venerunt.

(They), compelled by the messengers of Indutiomarus, a Treveri, incited their own people and suddenly, with the wood collectors having been hard pressed, they arrived with a great band at the camp to attack.

The supine in this sentence is “oppugnatum” and is set up by the verb of motion “venerunt”.

I. The Supine in -u:

The Supine in -u is used with a few adjectives to express an ablative of respect or specification.  It is more likely to be found in poetry over prose:

mirabile dictu

marvelous to say

facilis factu

easy to do

nihil dignum dictu

nothing worthy to mention

These forms of the supine are a little easier to recognize.  They are usually (not always) found with an adjective between commas or parentheses.

Consider Basic Sentence 20.3

Infert se saeptus nebula, mirabile dictu,

per medios, miscetque viris, neque cernitur ulli.  

He carried himself, enshrouded in a cloud, wonderful to say, through the middle (of them), and he mixed in with the men and wasn’t seen by anyone.            

The supine here is the word “dictu”.  You’ll notice that it is set up by the adjective “mirabile” (which is neuter and not describing anything) and is within commas.

The biggest difficulty people have is that supines (the -um version) look like perfect passive participles (PPPs).  Determining whether a word is a supine or PPP can be done by context and also looking to see if the word in question is describing a noun.  Remember, PPPs are adjectives, and supines are NOT!  Keep your eyes out for verbs of motion because this is another great contextual clue.
