Passive Voice

Up to this point we have only been using the active voice in Latin.  What this means is that the subject of the sentence is also the person or thing doing the action.  When the passive voice is used the object gets promoted to the subject and the former subject gets demoted (if even used at all) to a prepositional phrase.  Let’s look at an English example:

Wild penguins attacked my sister.

This sentence is active, because the wild penguins (the subject) are doing the action (attacked).

Prep Phrase

Using the passive voice in English is sometimes considered to be clunky and vague and therefore many textbook will tell you to avoid it.  This is because using the passive voice avoids having to tell you who or what did the action because the the agent can be left off.  For instance, Ronald Reagan was famous for saying, “Mistakes were made,” about the Iran Contra Affair.  Who made the mistakes?  Well, with the passive voice you don’t have to say who!  Some people say that businesses will use the passive voice too in order to seem less hostile to its customers.  For instance, if you fail to pay your electric bill, the power company will send you a letter saying that your power will be shut off.  It sounds less harsh than saying, ‘We will shut your power off.”

Latin however does not have this restriction.  The passive voice is used frequently and sometimes preferentially to put a focus on the action as opposed to the subject.

How do you know that a verb is passive?  You might recall that to form a verb you need to add the following endings on to tell the read who is doing the action (i.e. what person and number the verb is).

With these new endings you can tell the person, number, and now VOICE (active or passive) of a verb.  Let’s take a look at a basic sentence to practice:

10.3 Ea res Helvetiis per indicium enuntiatur.

This situation is announced to the Helvetians through evidence.

The word “enuntiatur” is our new passive form and you can see that the subject “res” is not actually doing the announcing.  In fact, in classic passive voice fashion, we don’t know who actually passed along this information.  Mostly likely this is because the person who gave it isn’t important, just the fact that it was passed along.  

Some students have trouble identifying whether their translation of a passive verb is actually passive.  One quick way is to use the “by pirates rule”

By Pirates Rule

If you can add “by pirates” to the end of a sentence and it makes sense, then your verb is probably passive.

Example:  I was attacked……..by pirates.   (this makes sense)

                  I attacked…….by pirates.  (nope.  This doesn’t make sense)

Passive infinitives: Do you remember when you learned about the principal parts of verbs?  The 2nd principal part is the infinitive, or the “to verb” form.  Infinitives can also be passive.  In 3 out of the 4 conjugations the rule for forming this is by chopping of the final “e” of the infinitive and adding an “i”.

Amare = to love

Amari = to be loved

The only other exception in 3rd conjugation.  In that conjugation the internal vowel is weak and will also get chopped off.  So in 3rd you remove the whole “ere” and then add your “i”.

Regere = to rule

Regi = to be ruled

Deponent Verbs

Some verbs in Latin have been crystallized in the passive form.  For some reason, through frequent use and years of misuse these verbs got stuck in the passive voice.  These verbs will ONLY appear passively and to top that off, these passive form are translated actively!

Deponent Verbs are passive verbs that translate actively

This can be annoying because how would you know if a verb is passive or deponent, because this would change your translation.  There are really two ways to prepare for this.  The first is to memorize the short list of these deponent verbs, most of which are vocabulary words for this chapter.  The other way is just through context.  How do these verbs look in the dictionary then, so I can recognize them?

Let’s try one:

Sequor, sequi, secutus sum = to follow

This pattern follows the dictionary pattern of previous verbs, though at first glance it may not seem like it.  The 1st principal part, is the 1st person singular “I” form, just as before.  So, “sequor” would be read as “I follow”.  The 2nd principal part is the infinitive, just as you learned above.  So, “sequor” is 3rd conjugation because the infinitive has just an “i” at the end.  The last principal part will be covered in a later chapter.  Let’s look at a Basic Sentence with a deponent verb:

10.1 Insequitur clamorque virorum stridorque rudentum.

The shouting of men and the creaking of ropes follows.

“Insequitur” is deponent.  It looks passive because of the ending “-tur” but it is translated actively.  Also notice the word order.  There is the ABAB order shown by the red green red green order.  This is called synchysis.  This can appear in poetry in English.  Here is an example:

I run and shoot, fast and accurate.

Agency vs. Instrument

Often times when a passive verb is used you get a prepositional phrase that follows to tell us who did the action.  What happens if that prepositional phrase is not describing a person, but instead is describing a thing?  

I was attacked by pirates.

Vs

I was attacked by gunfire.

Agency

Vs

Instrument

If it is a person then this is called an agent in Latin.  If it is not a person, then it is called an instrument in Latin. Why does this matter?  This matters because in Latin, if it is an agent, then you are going to use the preposition “a/ab” (by) followed by the ablative case.  If it is an instrument, then the preposition will be left off and you will use a NIAWOP.  Here’s an example:

Urbs a militibus vastatur.

The city is destroyed by the soldiers.

Agent

Urbs flammis vastatur.

The city is destroyed by flames.

Instrument
