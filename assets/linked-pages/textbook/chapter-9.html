The Future Tense (a house divided)

The future tense would have been just as easy to learn as the imperfect tense if it weren’t for the fact that there are two rules: one for 1st and 2nd conjugation verbs, and one for 3rd and 4th conjugation verbs.  Because of this it will be extremely important that you know how to identify the conjugation of any verb you meet.

Let’s start with the rule for 1st and 2nd conjugation:

1st and 2nd Conjugation Future Tense

Add a “bo, bi, bi, bi, bi, bu” between the stem and the ending of the verb.

Some like to picture this as a b + O4IsU (the vowels from 3rd conjugation)

Let’s see what this actually looks like on a verb:

“God will give an end to these things also.”

“Dabit” is the future form of the verb “do, dare” and translated as “will give” because of that.

Now the rule for 3rd and 4th conjugation:

3rd and 4th Conjugation Future Tense

Add an “a” in the 1st person singular or an “e” in all other forms between the stem and the ending of the verb.

The letter “e” is seen as the major marker for the future tense in these verbs.  It is harder to identify these forms because an “e” is more subtle than the “bo,bi,bi,bi,bi,bu” from the other rule.

These forms can often trick students and here is why.  Consider the word “bibet” (to drink).  If this is a verb you have never seen before you would not know whether it is a 2nd or 3rd conjugation verb.  If it were 2nd, the internal vowel “e” would just mark the normal present tense because the “e” is part of the stem (like videt).  If “bibet” were 3rd conjugation, then this “e” would mean that it is the future tense.  Unfortunately, the only way to know is either to look up the word in the dictionary or have memorized it.  Using the context around the word can also help.  The 1st person singular can also give students fits because of its similarity to noun endings.  Consider the following sentence:

Regam regiam.  

Both words end in “am”, but one is a verb and one is a noun!  Yikes!  This is where Latin starts to become more difficult for students.  The overlapping forms creates a lot of ambiguity.

Regam regiam.

I will rule the palace.

Relative Pronouns

Relative pronouns is a MAJOR topic.  It appears often in ALL Latin authors.  Mastery of this will be important to become good at Latin.  

Luckily we have already been introduced to the fact that pronouns have slightly different forms than nouns and have a few exceptions to remember.  “Qui, quae, quod” (who, which, whom, whose, that) is no different.  Before focusing on how to use these words we need to know what they look like:

Anytime you learn the forms of a new word and you have a new chart of forms to review, it is always best before just memorizing by brute force, to take a look at how the forms are the same and how they are different, both from other words and from within the word itself.   One of the things that jumps out to me is that it follows the two rules of pronouns but it changes its stem from a “q” to a “c” in those forms.  Also, the plural forms seem to be a hybrid between the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd declension, but MOST of the forms I would call “normal”.  So, sure, you can memorize this chart and it will help you, but it might be easier to just memorize how this chart is different.  

As far as meaning goes, please note that this word has many meanings.  It is most commonly used as a relative pronoun.  “Qui, quae, quod” can also be used as an interrogative adjective and start a question, but that is not within the scope of this chapter.

One last interesting note is that the relative pronoun in English is one of the few words in our Language that still reflect the case system that Latin uses.  For instance, have you ever noticed that we change the word “who” to “whom” when the word is the direct object?  Take a look at the word “whom”.  It ends with an “m” just like the accusative case in Latin!

Relative Clauses

So, now that we know the forms of this word, how does it get used?  A relative pronoun will start a relative clause.  Clauses are not new to this chapter, as we have seen them before with causal and temporal clauses.  When you see a form of “qui, quae, quod” you should start a bracket.  The clause will usually end at the next verb, but that is not always the case.  Sometimes the editors will help you by placing commas at the clause borders, but that also can’t always be counted on.  Try to use context to figure out where the clause ends for sure.  Let’s take a look at an English sentence first to familiarize ourselves with the structure.

I do not want to complete the homework, which is very long.

In the sentence above, the word “which” is a relative pronoun.  What has to be determined now is what the word “which” is referring to.  Luckily in English it is quite easy.  It is the noun immediately preceding the relative pronoun, “homework”.  Because English relies on word order to provide meaning, it is essential that they be next to one another.  However, in Latin, word order is more fluid.  So, how would I know what noun a pronoun is referring to in Latin?  Pronouns almost act like adjectives.  Remember that adjectives have to match the noun they are describing in case, number, and gender.  Relative pronouns almost work the same way.  They need to match in NUMBER and GENDER ONLY!!!

*************************************

RELATIVE PRONOUNS MATCH THEIR ANTECEDENTS IN NUMBER AND GENDER ONLY!

*************************************

The reason that this is true is because the relative pronoun is not merely describing a noun.  It also has a function of its own in its own clause.  Let’s take another look at our English sentence from above to see this in action.

I do not want to complete the homework, [which is very long].

In this sentence the word “homework” is the direct object of the main sentence.  If this sentence were in Latin, the word would be in the accusative case.  In the relative clause, the word “which” is the subject of a linking sentence.  If this were written in Latin, the word “which” would be nominative because it is the subject of a verb.  So, even though the relative pronoun is describing homework, their functions are quite different.  Let’s look at a Basic Sentence for some practice:

                                   

9.2 Erant omnino itinera duo, [quibus domo exire poterant.]

                                       

There were two paths entirely, [by which they were able to leave from (their) home.]

A lot is going on in this sentence and it is a great illustration of many new topics from this chapter and the last.  The most important thing to notice here is that the word “quibus” is describing “itinera”.   On the surface this seems unlikely because the endings don’t match.  But, remember that relative pronouns only need to match their antecedent in NUMBER and GENDER.  So, “itinera” is plural and neuter.  “Quibus” is also plural and neuter.  So the words match.  If you look at the cases of the two words , “itinera” is nominative because it the subject of the verb “erant”.  “Quibus” is ablative because is is expressing the “means” by which these people could leave their homeland, and is therefore acting as a NIAWOP.  We have provided the word “by” in the translation because NIAWOPs require one.

Let’s try another sentence to see it in another light:

Milites, [quos Caesar ducit], ad finem Italiae stant.

The soldiers, [whom Caesar leads], are standing at the border of Italy.

Again, notice that “quos” is describing “milites”.  They match in number and gender (masculine plural).  They do not match in case because their functions are different.  (note:  they can match in case, they just don’t have to).

N.B. When using the preposition “cum” with the word “qui, quae, quod” the words will combine to form one word and reverse order.  So, if I wanted to say “with whom” using the Latin words “cum quo” or “cum quibus”  the words would become “quocum” or “quibuscum” respectively.  This is also the case for some other words in Latin, like “mecum” and “tecum”.
