Perfect Tense

The perfect tense, sometimes called the present perfect tense in English, is going to finally use the 3rd and 4th principal parts of our dictionary entry.  Before we get into forms, let’s see how this tense is used in English.

As opposed to the imperfect tense, the perfect tense is an action that has been completed.  Because there are fewer tenses in Latin than in English, the perfect tense is used to express three verb tenses in English:

Latin Perfect Tense

English Tenses

Present Perfect

Simple Past

Past Emphatic

I have loved

I loved

I did love

So, the Latin perfect tense can be translated just as each of those verbs above.  You will have to use context to see which way fits best.  To keep things simple at first I would use HAS/HAVE (the first example above) when you see a perfect tense verb and as you get better, branch out into different interpretations.  It may sound awkward at times, but keeping it simple earlier on is important.

Now that we are using the 3rd principal part, it would be good to learn exactly what it is and what it is used for.  The 3rd principal part is the equivalent to the 1st principal part, but in the perfect tense.

3rd Principal Part is the 1st person singular perfect tense (I have verbed)

If you drop the “i” this gives the perfect stem which will be used for the base of all active perfect tense (and other) verbs.

Perfect Passive:

The perfect passive is even easier!  All you do is the following:

4th principal part + form of “sum”

It will be a two word verb!

N.B.  The first word of this verb will change endings to match the subject.  You’ll notice “amati” and “amatus” in the singular.  This could be different as well.  If the subject were a woman, then it would be “amata” instead.

To translate these forms we will keep the HAS/HAVE helping verb that was used in the active voice but to make it passive the word “been” needs to be added.

Example:

Amatus est = he has been loved.  (You could also say “he was loved”)

The “HAS/HAVE” indicates a perfect tense verb and the “BEEN” indicates that it’s passive.

Perfect Passive Participle

The perfect passive participle, from now on abbreviated PPP, is a verbal adjective just like the PAP which was introduced a few chapters ago.  The difference this time is that the action is completed (perfect) and it is passive instead of active.  The easiest part of learning this verb form is that you already have seen it a lot.  The PPP IS the 4th principle part of a verb’s dictionary entry.  That’s it.  Well, almost at least.  Remember, that because a participle is not only a verb, but also an adjective.  Therefore it will need to match the noun it is describing in case, number, and gender.  Because of this necessity it will need to have enough forms to match any noun it comes across.  The PAP from a prior chapter took 3rd declension endings.  In this instance, the PPP is going to take 1st, 2nd, or 2nd neuter endings just like a 1st and 2nd declension adjective.  It will take 1st declension endings if it needs to be feminine, 2nd declension endings if it needs to be masculine, and 2nd neuter if it needs to be neuter.  Here is an example of full PPP form chart:

Now that you can recognize and translate a PPP, let’s take a look at Basic Sentence 11.1.

                         

Eodem die legati ab hostibus missi ad Caesarem de pace venerunt.

In this sentence we start off with a NIAWOP (die) described by a pronoun (eodem). “Legati” is the subject in the nominative case.  It is immediately followed by a prepositional phrase (ab hostibus).  Next is new this chapter.  “Missi” is a PPP from the verb “mitto, mittere, misi, missus.  The ending on “missi” tells the reader that it is nominative and is describing “legati.  Two more prepositional phrases follow (ad Caesarem and de pace) and finally the sentence ends with the verb.  This verb form is also new this chapter.  It has an “-erunt” ending which makes it perfect tense.  This can be translated as “have arrived” or better here as just “arrived”.

                                   

On that same day, ambassadors, having been sent to Caesar about peace, arrived.

Idem, Eadem, Idem

Why, might you ask, does this pronoun deserve its own section?  It is because it is a bit irregular.  The chart is below.  One thing to notice is that it is almost as if someone took the pronoun “is, ea, id” and mashed the suffix “-dem” on the end of it.  Many students have trouble with this word because it looks like it has an accusative ending on it.  Consider Basic Sentence 11.1.  “Eodem” could easily be confused for an accusative word, but it is in fact ablative because “-dem” is a suffix and the ending is “-o”.
